Problem 1.

What is the probability that a driver will pass through point C while driving from place A to place B by using shortest routes on the given street layout?
Grid: A = (0,0), B = (6,5), C = (2,3).

Solution

On a rectangular grid, a shortest path from \((0,0)\) to \((6,5)\) consists of exactly \(6\) steps East (R) and \(5\) steps North (U), in any order. The total number of such shortest paths is \[ \binom{6+5}{6} = \binom{11}{6} = 462. \]

To pass through \(C=(2,3)\) a driver must first travel a shortest path from \(A=(0,0)\) to \(C=(2,3)\), then a shortest path from \(C=(2,3)\) to \(B=(6,5)\).

Paths A → C: 2 steps East, 3 steps North. \[ \binom{2+3}{2} = \binom{5}{2} = 10. \]

Paths C → B: \((6-2)=4\) steps East, \((5-3)=2\) steps North. \[ \binom{4+2}{4} = \binom{6}{4} = 15. \]

Paths through C: \[ 10 \times 15 = 150. \]

Probability: \[ P(C) = \frac{150}{462} = \frac{25}{77} \approx 0.3247. \]

\(\displaystyle P(\text{pass through }C) = \frac{25}{77} \approx 32.47\%\)
Problem 2.

Six Stevens faculty members are randomly surveyed from 11 equally likely departments. What is the probability that at least two are from the same department?

Solution

It is easier to use the complement: \(P(\text{at least two same}) = 1 - P(\text{all different})\).

Each of the 6 faculty members is independently assigned to one of 11 departments. Total equally likely outcomes: \[ 11^6 = 1{,}771{,}561. \]

Outcomes where all 6 are from different departments (choosing 6 distinct departments out of 11, ordered): \[ 11 \times 10 \times 9 \times 8 \times 7 \times 6 = \frac{11!}{5!} = 332{,}640. \]

\[ P(\text{all different}) = \frac{332{,}640}{1{,}771{,}561} \approx 0.1879. \]

\[ P(\text{at least two same}) = 1 - 0.1879 \approx 0.8121. \]

\(\displaystyle P(\text{at least two from same dept}) = 1 - \frac{11\cdot10\cdot9\cdot8\cdot7\cdot6}{11^6} = \frac{1{,}438{,}921}{1{,}771{,}561} \approx 81.2\%\)
Problem 3.

A die is rolled five times. What is the probability of obtaining all different numbers on the five rolls, given that the first and last rolls add to 5?

Solution

Let \(E\) = "all five rolls are different" and \(F\) = "first roll + last roll = 5". We want \(P(E \mid F)\).

Find \(P(F)\):
Pairs \((d_1, d_5)\) with \(d_1+d_5=5\): \((1,4),(2,3),(3,2),(4,1)\). That is 4 pairs. \[ P(F) = \frac{4 \times 6^3}{6^5} = \frac{4}{36} = \frac{1}{9}. \] (The middle three rolls are free; each pair has probability \(1/36\).)

Find \(P(E \cap F)\):
We need all 5 rolls distinct AND \(d_1+d_5=5\). The valid pairs \((d_1,d_5)\) are the same 4 pairs above (note all pairs have distinct values). For each such pair, the 3 middle rolls must be distinct and different from both \(d_1\) and \(d_5\). There are \(6-2=4\) remaining values, so the middle 3 rolls must be 3 distinct values chosen from those 4: \[ 4 \times 3 \times 2 = 24 \text{ ordered choices}. \] Number of favorable outcomes: \(4 \times 24 = 96\). \[ P(E \cap F) = \frac{96}{6^5} = \frac{96}{7776} = \frac{2}{162} = \frac{1}{81}. \]

Conditional probability: \[ P(E \mid F) = \frac{P(E \cap F)}{P(F)} = \frac{96/7776}{4 \cdot 216/7776} = \frac{96}{864} = \frac{2}{18} = \frac{1}{9}. \]

\(\displaystyle P(\text{all different} \mid d_1+d_5=5) = \frac{96}{864} = \frac{1}{9} \approx 11.1\%\)
Problem 4.

A bag contains 6 red and 5 blue balls (11 total). Three balls are drawn at random. What is the probability of drawing at least one red ball?

Solution

Use the complement: \(P(\text{at least one red}) = 1 - P(\text{no red}) = 1 - P(\text{all blue})\).

Total ways to choose 3 from 11: \[ \binom{11}{3} = 165. \]

Ways to choose 3 blue balls from 5: \[ \binom{5}{3} = 10. \]

\[ P(\text{all blue}) = \frac{10}{165} = \frac{2}{33}. \]

\[ P(\text{at least one red}) = 1 - \frac{2}{33} = \frac{31}{33}. \]

\(\displaystyle P(\text{at least one red}) = \frac{31}{33} \approx 93.9\%\)
Problem 5.

Let \(E\) and \(F\) be events with \(Pr(E)=0.7\), \(Pr(F)=0.5\), and \(Pr(F\mid E)=0.6\). Find \(Pr(E \cup F)\).

Solution

Step 1 – Find \(Pr(E \cap F)\) using the definition of conditional probability: \[ Pr(F \mid E) = \frac{Pr(E \cap F)}{Pr(E)} \implies Pr(E \cap F) = 0.6 \times 0.7 = 0.42. \]

Step 2 – Apply the inclusion-exclusion formula: \[ Pr(E \cup F) = Pr(E) + Pr(F) - Pr(E \cap F) = 0.7 + 0.5 - 0.42 = 0.78. \]

\(\displaystyle Pr(E \cup F) = 0.78\)
Problem 6.

Let \(A\) and \(B\) be independent events with \(Pr(A)=0.5\) and \(Pr(B)=0.6\). What is the probability that neither \(A\) nor \(B\) occurs?

Solution

We want \(Pr(A^c \cap B^c) = Pr((A \cup B)^c) = 1 - Pr(A \cup B)\).

Since \(A\) and \(B\) are independent: \[ Pr(A \cap B) = Pr(A) \cdot Pr(B) = 0.5 \times 0.6 = 0.30. \]

\[ Pr(A \cup B) = 0.5 + 0.6 - 0.30 = 0.80. \]

Equivalently, since \(A^c\) and \(B^c\) are also independent: \[ Pr(A^c \cap B^c) = Pr(A^c)\cdot Pr(B^c) = (1-0.5)(1-0.6) = 0.5 \times 0.4 = 0.20. \]

\(\displaystyle Pr(\text{neither } A \text{ nor } B) = 0.20\)
