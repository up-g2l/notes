MA 574 (F25) — Bayes' Theorem: Medical Test

Problem.

A medical test for a rare disease has the following properties:

  • The disease affects 0.5% of the population.
  • If a person has the disease, the test is positive with probability 0.99 (sensitivity).
  • If a person does not have the disease, the test is positive with probability 0.02 (false positive rate).

If a randomly selected person tests positive, what is the probability that they actually have the disease?

Setup — Define Events

Let \(D\) = "person has the disease" and \(T^+\) = "test result is positive." We are given: \[ P(D) = 0.005, \quad P(D^c) = 0.995, \] \[ P(T^+ \mid D) = 0.99 \quad \text{(true positive rate / sensitivity)}, \] \[ P(T^+ \mid D^c) = 0.02 \quad \text{(false positive rate)}. \] We want \(P(D \mid T^+)\).

Step 1 — Total Probability of a Positive Test

By the Law of Total Probability: \[ P(T^+) = P(T^+ \mid D)\,P(D) + P(T^+ \mid D^c)\,P(D^c). \] \[ P(T^+) = (0.99)(0.005) + (0.02)(0.995) = 0.00495 + 0.01990 = 0.02485. \]

Step 2 — Apply Bayes' Theorem

\[ P(D \mid T^+) = \frac{P(T^+ \mid D)\,P(D)}{P(T^+)} = \frac{(0.99)(0.005)}{0.02485} = \frac{0.00495}{0.02485} \approx 0.1992. \]

Intuition — A Table of 100,000 People

Imagining a population of 100,000 makes the numbers concrete:

Has Disease \((D)\) No Disease \((D^c)\) Total
Test Positive \((T^+)\) 495 1,990 2,485
Test Negative \((T^-)\) 5 97,510 97,515
Total 500 99,500 100,000

Of the 2,485 people who test positive, only 495 actually have the disease: \[ P(D \mid T^+) = \frac{495}{2{,}485} \approx 19.9\%. \]

\(\displaystyle P(D \mid T^+) = \frac{0.00495}{0.02485} \approx 0.1992 \approx 19.9\%\)

Key takeaway: Even with a highly sensitive test (99%), the positive predictive value is only about 20% because the disease is rare (0.5%). The large pool of healthy people produces many false positives, swamping the true positives. This is a classic consequence of Bayes' Theorem applied to rare-event screening.
